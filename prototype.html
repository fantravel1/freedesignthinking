Prototyping in design thinking is the process of creating quick, low-cost representations of your ideas to test and refine them. Prototypes range from simple paper sketches to interactive digital mockups. The goal is to fail fast and learn quickly - spending minimal time and resources to validate concepts before full development.
Low-fidelity prototypes are quick, rough representations (paper sketches, cardboard models, sticky notes) used early in the design process to explore concepts. High-fidelity prototypes closely resemble the final product with detailed visuals and functionality, used later for user testing and stakeholder buy-in. Start low-fi to iterate quickly, then increase fidelity as you refine.
Common rapid prototyping materials include:
  • Cardboard and foam core: Great for 3D models and physical products
  • Paper and sticky notes: Perfect for interface and service mockups
  • LEGO: Excellent for modular concepts and space design
  • Digital tools (Figma, Sketch): Ideal for app and web prototypes
Follow the "minimum viable prototype" principle - spend just enough time to test your hypothesis. Early prototypes might take 15-30 minutes. More refined prototypes could take a few hours. If you're spending days on a prototype, you're likely over-engineering. Remember: the goal is learning, not perfection.
A storyboard prototype is a sequence of drawings or images that illustrate how a user interacts with your solution over time. Like a comic strip, it shows the user's journey, emotions, and key touchpoints. Storyboards are excellent for communicating service experiences, app flows, and scenarios that unfold across multiple steps.
Move to building when: 1) User testing consistently validates your core concept, 2) You've iterated enough to understand key requirements, 3) Stakeholders are aligned on the direction, 4) The remaining questions require a functional product to answer. Don't move too early - the cost of changes increases dramatically once you start building.
