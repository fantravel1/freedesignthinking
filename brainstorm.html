Brainstorming is the ideation phase of the design thinking process where teams generate a wide range of creative ideas without judgment. The goal is divergent thinking - producing as many ideas as possible before converging on solutions. Effective brainstorming encourages wild ideas, builds on others' suggestions, and defers judgment to create an open, creative environment.
The core rules of brainstorming, developed at IDEO and Stanford d.school, include:
  • Defer judgment: No criticism or evaluation during ideation
  • Encourage wild ideas: The crazier the better - they can be tamed later
  • Build on others' ideas: Use "Yes, and..." to expand concepts
  • Go for quantity: Aim for 100+ ideas, not perfect ones
  • One conversation at a time: Stay focused and listen
  • Be visual: Sketch and draw ideas, don't just talk
  • Stay on topic: Keep the problem statement in focus
A productive brainstorming session should aim for at least 50-100 ideas within 30-60 minutes. Research from the Stanford d.school shows that the best ideas often come after the obvious ones are exhausted - typically after idea #40 or #50. This is why quantity matters: pushing past the easy answers leads to truly innovative solutions.
Divergent thinking is the creative, expansive phase where you generate many possibilities without judgment. It's about exploring widely and considering unconventional ideas. Convergent thinking comes after, where you evaluate, combine, and select the most promising ideas. Effective design thinking requires both: first diverge to explore the problem space, then converge to focus on solutions.
Popular team brainstorming techniques include:
  • Crazy 8s: Sketch 8 ideas in 8 minutes to force rapid ideation
  • Mind Mapping: Visual expansion of concepts and connections
  • SCAMPER: Systematic prompts to modify existing ideas
  • Brainwriting: Silent written ideation to include introverts
  • How Might We: Reframe problems as opportunity questions
  • Worst Possible Idea: Generate terrible ideas to spark creativity
Optimal brainstorming sessions last 30-60 minutes. Shorter sessions (15-20 minutes) work well for focused techniques like Crazy 8s. Research shows creativity tends to decline after 60-90 minutes, so it's better to take breaks and run multiple shorter sessions. Always include a 5-10 minute warm-up activity to get participants in a creative mindset before the main brainstorming begins.
"Yes, and..." is an improv technique that encourages building on ideas rather than shutting them down. When someone says "Yes, and..." they accept the previous idea and add to it, creating psychological safety and momentum. This contrasts with "No, but..." or "That won't work because..." which kills creativity. Teams using "Yes, and..." generate 40% more ideas and report higher engagement during brainstorming sessions.
