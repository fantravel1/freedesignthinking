Design Thinking Brainstorming

Master brainstorming in design thinking with proven ideation techniques. Learn how to generate more creative ideas, facilitate effective brainstorm sessions, and turn concepts into prototypes.

Quick Answer

Brainstorming in design thinking is the ideation phase where teams generate as many ideas as possible without judgment. Key principles include: defer judgment, encourage wild ideas, build on others' ideas, and go for quantity. Popular techniques include SCAMPER, mind mapping, Crazy 8s, and the worst possible idea method. Aim for 50-100 ideas in a 30-minute session.

What is Brainstorming in Design Thinking?

Brainstorming in design thinking is the ideation phase that follows "Define" and precedes "Prototype." It's where teams generate a wide range of creative solutions to the problem they've identified. Unlike casual brainstorming, design thinking brainstorming follows specific principles to maximize creativity.

The goal isn't to find the perfect idea immediately‚Äîit's to generate as many ideas as possible. Research shows that the best ideas often come after the obvious ones have been exhausted. By going for quantity, you push past conventional thinking into more innovative territory.

How to Facilitate a Design Thinking Brainstorm

  1. Warm up first: Start with a 5-10 minute creative exercise to shift participants into a creative mindset and build team energy.
  2. Frame the challenge: Present a clear "How Might We" question based on insights from your Define phase. The framing is crucial‚Äîtoo broad and you get unfocused ideas; too narrow and you limit creativity.
  3. Set ground rules: Review the 7 rules of brainstorming. Post them visibly. Remind people that there are no bad ideas at this stage.
  4. Provide materials: Give everyone sticky notes and markers. Standing at a wall or whiteboard encourages energy and movement.
  5. Set a timer: 15-30 minutes is ideal. Time pressure prevents overthinking and keeps energy high.
  6. Encourage building: When someone shares an idea, others should build on it with "Yes, and..." rather than "No, but..."
  7. Capture everything: Write every idea on a sticky note. One idea per note makes clustering easier later.
  8. Cluster and vote: After brainstorming, group similar ideas into themes. Use dot voting to identify the most promising concepts to prototype.

Common Brainstorming Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping the warm-up: Jumping straight into brainstorming often produces conventional ideas. Warm-ups prime creative thinking.
  • Allowing criticism: Even subtle judgment ("that's interesting, but...") shuts down creative risk-taking.
  • Settling too early: Don't stop when you have "enough" ideas. Push through the obvious to find unexpected solutions.
  • One person dominating: Use techniques like brainwriting or round-robin to ensure everyone contributes.
  • Vague framing: A poorly framed challenge leads to scattered ideas. Invest time in crafting the right "How Might We" question.

From Brainstorming to Prototyping

Brainstorming doesn't end with a wall full of sticky notes. The next step is selecting ideas to prototype. Use voting methods like dot voting (each person gets 3 dots to place on their favorite ideas) or the four categories method (most likely to delight, most innovative, most practical, long shot).

Remember: in design thinking, you prototype to learn, not to prove you're right. Choose ideas that you're genuinely curious about, even if you're not sure they'll work. The fastest way to find out is to build something and test it.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many ideas should we generate in a brainstorm?

+
Aim for 50-100 ideas in a 30-minute session for a small group (4-6 people). Stanford d.school emphasizes "going for volume" because breakthrough ideas often come after obvious ones are exhausted. Don't worry about quality at this stage‚Äîyou can always narrow down later.

What's the difference between brainstorming and ideation?

+
Brainstorming is one specific technique within the broader ideation phase. Ideation encompasses all methods for generating ideas, including brainstorming, sketching, prototyping concepts, and bodystorming. Brainstorming typically refers to group verbal/written idea generation, while ideation includes individual and physical methods too.

How do you brainstorm with introverts?

+
Use techniques like brainwriting (silent written brainstorming), give advance notice of the topic so people can prepare, start with individual ideation before group sharing, and create a psychologically safe environment where all contributions are valued equally.

What is a "How Might We" question?

+
"How Might We" (HMW) questions reframe challenges as opportunities for design. They're broad enough to allow creative solutions but focused enough to provide direction. Example: Instead of "Users don't exercise," try "How might we make exercise feel like play for busy parents?"

Can you brainstorm alone?

+
Yes! Solo brainstorming (sometimes called "brainwriting") can be very effective. Use techniques like mind mapping, SCAMPER, or timed free-writing. Some studies suggest initial solo ideation followed by group building produces better results than pure group brainstorming.
